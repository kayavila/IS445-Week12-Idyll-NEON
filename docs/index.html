Introduction

The National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON) is a thirty-year National Science Foundation (NSF) project. It gathers ecological data, processes it, and makes it freely available to scientists and the public at large.

This data comes from three different types of sources:

aerial sensors
“Airborne Remote Sensing” data is collected by the NEON Airborne Observation Platform (AOP). See https://www.neonscience.org/data-collection/airborne-remote-sensing for more information.
,
automated ground sensors
The sensors used to collect this data are called “Automated Instruments”. See https://www.neonscience.org/data-collection/automated-instruments for more information.
, and
data collected by hand
For more information on this “Observational Sampling”, see https://www.neonscience.org/data-collection/observational-sampling.
.

Beetle Pitfall Traps

One of the manually collected data products comes from pitfall traps designed to capture ground beetles.

The image to the right comes from the NEON website’s page about pitfall traps. It a photo of one of the pitfall traps being installed. They are designed for the animals to fall into them and be preserved by glycol in the bottom of the trap. The traps are set and emptied every two weeks during the capture season, at each of the capture sites.

The traps are focused on capturing beetles from the family Carabidae. This family is common and highly indicative of the overall ecological health of the system.

Although the traps are positioned low to the ground in order to minimize the other species inadvertently caught, other invertebrates and small vertebrates are also sometimes captured. These are referred to as “bycatch”.

Data

I downloaded the beetle pitfall data from NEON’s site. The downloaded data is separated by month and site location, so I used NEON’s neonUtilities package to “stack” the data into a single csv file per type of data.

I was curious about the bycatch data, so I explored the different datasets in a Jupyter notebook, and honed into the bet-fielddata.csv file. I saved the stacked version of this as data/processed-beetle.csv in GitHub so I could access it in a Starboard notebook and develop the vegalite graphs embedded here.

The processed-beetle.csv data is also copied into the idyll data/ directory.
